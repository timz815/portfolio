Hangout Calendar

HangoutCalendar is a cross-platform scheduling app that reduces the time and cognitive load students face when coordinating group meetings.

It shows group members' overlapping free time and users' personal calendars, giving users an easy way to confirm meeting times with reminders.

The Process

Problem Space

Coordinating group meetings can be messy and frustrating, especially when participants have conflicting schedules. For instance, university students, whose schedules often vary due to different classes and commitments, can find it difficult to coordinate a time that works for everyone.

Existing tools only partially address this problem. Google Calendar and Outlook focus mainly on individual scheduling, while group scheduling solutions such as Calendly are either geared towards individual appointment bookings or unintuitive and not optimized for group scheduling workflows. As a result, students are left without an efficient way to coordinate group meetings.

Opportunity

Students could benefit from a tool that helps groups efficiently find meeting times and reduce scheduling friction.

Research + Insights

To understand how students currently coordinate group meetings, we conducted informal interviews with classmates and friends and gathered feedback on early prototypes.

Our main takeaways were:

Users value social boundaries and privacy

Participants wanted to keep calendar events and details separate across different social circles, such as friends, classmates, and family. What they were comfortable sharing depended on the group.

Users want real-time clarity without real-time participation

Because large groups cannot realistically coordinate availability simultaneously, responses arrive asynchronously, causing members to develop different understandings of the group’s availability and making plans increasingly difficult to finalize.

Based on these findings, we created a current user journey to capture how students coordinate meetings and identify points of friction.

Design Focus

Thus, our driving question became: How might we help groups quickly identify and confirm shared availability without requiring real-time response, while preserving separation between social contexts in an intuitive way?

Iterations

We began by organizing group scheduling into dedicated group spaces, where members could view shared availability and confirm meeting times in one place. This helped consolidate scattered scheduling information into one shared context which reduced the number of coordination steps and allowed for faster decision making.

In earlier versions, we displayed both group events and unavailable times as event blocks in a shared group calendar timetable.

While this created a complete overview, internal usability evaluation showed that it also introduced excessive visual clutter and made both group events and unavailable time slots slower to scan and interpret.

To reduce visual complexity, we separated these two needs: the group page prioritized scheduled events, since users mainly open a group to see what has already been scheduled within that group, while availability data appeared only when creating a new event, where it directly supports the task of comparing possible meeting times.

Another iteration focused on the week-to-month calendar transition. In reviewing existing calendar app plugins, we found many calendar interfaces handled this transition poorly.

To make the interaction feel more continuous, we based the gesture model on physical calendars. Similar to flipping pages in a calendar, horizontal swiping moves users forward and backward in time, such as swiping right to check the next week. Vertical gestures adjusted the level of detail between week and month views.

This consistent gesture direction keeps navigation predictable, aligns with user intent, and additionally accommodates months of varying length smoothly.

Solution Briefing

Hangout Calendar App

Building from these iterations, the final design focuses on two core patterns: group-based scheduling spaces and a synced personal calendar.

Below are the core interface patterns of the final design:

Familiar Interaction Patterns

Although HangoutCalendar introduces a new approach to group scheduling, it is grounded in familiar interaction patterns to reduce onboarding friction. By borrowing conventions from messaging and social apps, such as profile-based identity, adding contacts, and bottom tab navigation, users can quickly move between personal calendars, group calendars, and their profile without learning a new mental model.

Group-based Seperation

Scheduling happens within group spaces, where users can add members, similar to how conversations are organized in messaging apps. Within each group, users can view and manage plans specific to that group, which makes commitments within the group clear and easy to understand.

When creating a new event, availability is presented as a timetable with unavailable time slots marked in red. This is derived from each member’s personal calendar, so shared free slots can be identified without revealing each individual commitment. Events can then be confirmed directly within the group.

Synced Personal Calendar

The personal calendar tab serves as a dashboard for users to view all their commitments and personal schedule in one place. Continuous syncing with group calendars keeps it up to date automatically.

Locking Back...

Working under a tight deadline requires our team to be deliberate about our prioritization and scope. Early on the project, we defined clear boundaries around what was essential to demo HangoutCalendar's core value from nice to have features. This gave us a framework for making tradeoffs and balancing our ambition with realistic execution as constraints emerged.

As the project progressed, we set clear cutoffs for feature exploration and used the roadmap to decide when ideas should be simplified or deferred. We prioritized delivering a reliable, synced calendar with simplified block event entries over implementing a full hour-by-hour vertical timetable day view, which was unlikely to be completed on time and deferred features like a dedicated notifications tab or colour coded calendars when they risked pulling focus from smoothing out core feature interactions.

This approach allowed us to deliver a cohesive, fully functioning app within the timeframe and successfully demonstrate the intended user experience and core interactions. It reinforced for me the value of focusing on user-facing outcomes rather than feature completeness, especially when working in a team setting under tight deadlines.
